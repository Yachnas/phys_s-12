Design


Our second lesson covers creating two-dimensional and three-dimensional models using CAD software. Modeling complex shapes and solids is crucial to digital fabrication, as vector CAD files instruct fablab tools ranging from laser cutters to 3d printers. These versatile tools enable countless possibilities for independent fabrication, minimizing reliance on private entities for manufactured products and alleviating pressure on the environment.

Measurements


For this assignment I chose to model a plastic cup I found in my household and an AA battery from our kit. I first prepared my caliper by turning it on, shifting the outside jaw to its default position at the beginning of the ruler, and pressing the zero button to correct its slight initial inaccuracy. Since the caliper came preconfigured to use millimeters I did not need to adjust its unit of measurement with the unit button. I gathered the plastic cup and the AA battery and proceeded to measure them carefully with the caliper.



After recording the cup radii, cup height, cup thickness, battery base, battery height and battery terminal measurements I proceeded to create a sketch of each object to get an idea for how I would replicate them using the techniques I learned from class and from viewing the aforementioned beginner tutorials.

Cup Model


To model the cup I would need to find a way to create a hollow cylindrical solid. I searched through tutorials and found that I could draw a silhouette of the cup and rotate it about the origin to create the cylindrical solid. I could then use the shell modify tool to hollow the solid and finalize the cup model.

This image shows me drawing the silhouette of the cup. I used a line tool to draw the straight portions of the cup silhouette, and I used a spline tool to approximate the ridged exterior of the cup. I switched from using the conventional spline tool to the spline with control points tool because I thought it made more realistic looking ridges. I made sure to use the dimensions outlined on the cup sketch to create a model that would mirror its real life counterpart.

This image shows me using the revolve tool to rotate the shape formed by the lines in the previous step 360 degrees about the y-axis.

This image shows me using a shell tool to hollow the interior of the subsequent solid formed by applying the revolve tool. Like in the first step, I ensure that the thickness of the material matches the dimension outlined in the cup sketch.

This image shows me nearing the end of the cup modeling process. To make the model look more like a cup I change its render material to glass. I tried using plastic materials, but they looked too opaque. I tried using different color variants of glass but the plain one looked the most accurate so I stuck with it.

This image illustrates the final render of the cup model. Overall I am pretty happy with the final outcome. I think modeling the cup helped me a lot with familiarizing myself with the user interface of Fusion 360 as well as using basic features to create simple solids.

Battery Model


To model the battery I could build off the practice I got from modeling the cup, and I could also try to use newer tools like the hole tool and the component-assembly system in Fusion 360. For the model itself I could create a rectangular prism solid and fillet its corners to imitate the appearance of the battery. I could then create and model each of the terminals separately. Finally I would merge all three components into a single assembly file to finalize the battery model.

This image shows me creating the sketch for the battery base. I outlined where the terminals would be placed with construction lines, using the dimensions outlined in the battery sketch.

This image shows the result of extruding the sketch by the height of the battery base (roughly 44.67 mm).

This image documents the beginning of the positive battery terminal. Like the cup model, I imitate the straight bottom and top edges of the terminal with the line tool and use the spline tool to approximate its curved exterior.

Next I use the revolve tool to rotate the shape formed by the lines 360 degrees about the y-axis.

I use a shell tool to hollow the resulting solid and form the interior of the positive battery terminal. I could not get an accurate measurement of the thickness of the positive battery terminal, so I just tried to match the thickness to what I could see at a glance from observing the battery.

I fillet the interior bottom edge of the solid to the point where it forms a smaller circular area at the bottom where I could create another hole.

I extrude that resulting circular area by -0.25 mm to create the aforementioned hole. This finalizes the positive terminal component of the battery assembly.

This image documents the beginning of the negative battery terminal. I sketch a construction line with the width of the negative battery terminal before sketching a hexagon shape of the same width. I make sure to follow the measurements specified in the battery sketch.

Next I extrude the hexagon by the height of the negative battery terminal and fillet all of its edges.

After filleting the edges I use the hole modification tool to drill a hole at the center of the negative battery terminal with a flat bottom. I would then once again fillet the bottom edge of the new hole to create a smaller circular area at the bottom like in the positive battery terminal.

I use a hole tool again to bore an even smaller hole into the new small circular area.

Afterwards I extrude the smaller hole by less than one millimeter and bore another hole into it with before filleting the bottom edge of that hole to finalize the negative battery terminal.

With the battery base, negative battery terminal and positive battery terminal components finished, I could put them together in an assembly to create the battery. I changed each file to read-only to prevent myself from accidentally messing with the objects.

I moved each component around, using the construction lines of the battery base sketch as guidelines for where to position the terminals. Once I moved each terminal into the appropriate position the battery model was finished.

This image illustrates the final render of the battery model.
